## 3. Inaccuracies a top expert would flag in *any* application

1. **Confusing “digital health” with “health tech product”**  
   The taxonomy is about **investment and funding** (how money is classified, tracked, aligned). Building or selling a digital health *product* is relevant only if you also have experience in **funding, budgeting, or costing** of digital health (e.g. you costed a national strategy, or analysed budget lines for digital health).

2. **Overclaiming taxonomy experience**  
   Saying you “developed taxonomies” when you mean “I use ICD-10/DSM-5” (clinical classifications) is misleading. **Investment taxonomies** (e.g. how to classify and track *spending*) are different. If your experience is in *health* classifications (e.g. disease codes), say so clearly and only link to *investment* taxonomy if you have done that (e.g. mapping budget lines to categories).

3. **Vague “global” or “multi-country” experience**  
   The role is **four countries**, with **national** testing and **KIIs**. “I have worked in 10 countries” is useful only if you specify: in which countries you have **direct experience with government budgets, strategies, or stakeholder engagement**, and whether any of that was on **digital health or health financing**.

4. **References and citations**  
   If you cite reports (e.g. World Bank, OECD, WHO), use **correct titles, years, and links** if possible. A senior reviewer may check. Wrong year or misattributed claim (e.g. “WHO says X” when it was a partner report) undermines credibility.

5. **Budget arithmetic**  
   Your **total** (daily rate × number of days) must be **within $6,500–8,000**. If you propose 15 days, that implies a **max daily rate of ~$530**. State clearly: “Daily rate: $X; Estimated days: Y; Total: $Z (within the stated budget range).”
